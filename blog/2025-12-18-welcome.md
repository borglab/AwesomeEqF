> **Date:** 2025-12-18  
> **Author:** Rohan Bansal

Imagine a robot carrying a gyroscope and a 3-axis magnetometer. As the robot rotates, we want to keep track of the direction of the magnetic field in the robot's body frame. That direction is a unit vector

$$
\eta \in \mathbb{S}^2.
$$

The gyroscope tells us the angular velocity $\Omega$, and the direction evolves according to

$$
\dot{\eta} = -\Omega^\times \eta.
$$

The magnetometer gives us a measurement proportional to that direction:

$$
y = c_m \eta.
$$

This is a nice toy problem because it is easy to picture and already contains the whole difficulty. The state does not live in ordinary Euclidean space. It lives on the sphere.

That means a vanilla EKF has to choose between two slightly awkward options:

1. use local coordinates on the sphere
2. embed the sphere in $\mathbb{R}^3$ and enforce the unit-length constraint separately

Both approaches can work. But they both feel like we are bending the problem to fit the filter.

The **Equivariant Filter (EqF)** starts from the opposite direction. Instead of asking "which coordinates should I force onto this state space?", it asks:

> What symmetry is already present in the problem, and can I build the filter from that?

For the sphere example, the symmetry is rotation. Rotations in $\mathrm{SO}(3)$ move one direction on $\mathbb{S}^2$ to any other. The paper's big idea is to use that symmetry to define the observer, the error, and the linearization.

That is the point of the EqF paper by van Goor, Hamel, and Mahony. It gives a Kalman-style filter construction that respects the geometry of the problem instead of fighting it.

If you want to follow along with the original source, the paper is here: [Equivariant Filter (EqF)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.14666).

## The Short Version

If you only remember one idea from this post, make it this:

> The EqF chooses the estimation error using the natural group action of the problem, then linearizes that error around a fixed origin.

That already hints at why EqF is attractive:

- the error is geometric rather than ad hoc
- the linearization point is fixed rather than sliding around with the estimate
- the construction works on **homogeneous spaces**, not just on Lie groups

And that last point is the real leap beyond the IEKF.

The **Invariant Extended Kalman Filter (IEKF)** is great when the state itself lives on a Lie group such as $\mathrm{SO}(3)$ or $\mathrm{SE}(3)$. The EqF extends the same symmetry-first philosophy to state spaces like the sphere $\mathbb{S}^2$, which are not Lie groups themselves but still have a transitive symmetry action.

## Why This Example Is Already Hard

If you are new to filtering on manifolds, it is worth pausing here. Nothing about the sphere example sounds especially advanced. We are only tracking one direction vector.

But the geometry changes everything.

There is no single global linear coordinate system for $\mathbb{S}^2$. If we choose local coordinates, we inherit chart singularities and extra nonlinearities. If we work in $\mathbb{R}^3$, we have to remember that not every vector in $\mathbb{R}^3$ is a valid state, because the real state must stay on the unit sphere.

This is why EqF is interesting even before we get to its math. It is not trying to be clever for its own sake. It is trying to solve a genuine modeling problem:

> how do we define an estimation error that actually belongs to the geometry of the state space?

The paper's answer is: use symmetry to define the error first, and only then choose local coordinates to linearize it.

## The Geometric Setting

The paper works with systems whose state lives on a **homogeneous space**.

That phrase sounds abstract, but the idea is simple. A space $\mathcal{M}$ is homogeneous under a Lie group $G$ if every point in $\mathcal{M}$ can be moved to every other point by the action of some element of $G$.

Examples:

- $\mathbb{S}^2$ is homogeneous under rotations in $\mathrm{SO}(3)$
- orientations are homogeneous under $\mathrm{SO}(3)$ acting on itself
- poses are homogeneous under $\mathrm{SE}(3)$

So even if the state space is not itself a group, it may still inherit a lot of useful structure from a group action.

The paper writes this action as

$$
\phi : G \times \mathcal{M} \to \mathcal{M}.
$$

You can think of $\phi(X, \xi)$ as "apply symmetry element $X$ to state $\xi$."

## From Invariance to Equivariance

This is a good place to separate two words that are easy to blur together.

### Invariance

Very loosely, invariance means the system looks the same after a symmetry transformation.

### Equivariance

Equivariance is slightly more general. It means the state, inputs, or outputs transform in a compatible way under the group action.

For the EqF paper, the dynamics are called equivariant if there is also an input action

$$
\psi : G \times \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{L}
$$

such that transforming the state and transforming the input commute with the dynamics.

That sounds abstract, but the intuition is straightforward:

> If I re-express the same physical situation in a symmetry-related frame, the dynamics should transform consistently rather than becoming a different system.

This is the structure the EqF exploits.

## The Four Ingredients Behind EqF

The paper is built around four objects. Once these are in place, the rest of the construction becomes much easier to read.

### 1. A group action on the state space

We need a Lie group $G$ acting transitively on the state space $\mathcal{M}$ through $\phi$.

This tells us what the natural symmetry of the problem is.

### 2. An equivariant system

The state evolves according to

$$
\dot{\xi} = f_u(\xi),
$$

and the dynamics should respect the symmetry via the input action $\psi$.

### 3. A lift into the Lie algebra

This is one of the most important constructions in the paper. The authors introduce a map

$$
\Lambda : \mathcal{M} \times \mathcal{L} \to \mathfrak{g}
$$

called a **lift**.

The lift converts the system dynamics on the state space into an infinitesimal motion on the symmetry group. In less formal language:

> instead of evolving the observer directly on the manifold, we evolve a group element whose projection onto the manifold matches the state trajectory

This is how EqF gets an observer that lives on the group even when the true state does not.

### 4. An equivariant output, if available

If the output map also respects the symmetry, the paper gets an extra bonus. There exists an output action

$$
\rho : G \times \mathcal{N} \to \mathcal{N}
$$

such that

$$
\rho(X, h(\xi)) = h(\phi(X, \xi)).
$$

That extra structure is what leads to the improved EqF$^\star$ measurement linearization later on.

## The Central Trick: Put the Observer on the Group

This is where the construction gets elegant.

Pick a fixed origin $\xi^\circ \in \mathcal{M}$. Then define the observer state to be a group element $\hat{X} \in G$, and recover the state estimate by projecting it back to the manifold:

$$
\hat{\xi} = \phi(\hat{X}, \xi^\circ).
$$

So the observer is not directly storing a point on the state space. It is storing the symmetry transformation that moves the chosen origin to the current estimate.

This is especially useful on spaces like $\mathbb{S}^2$, where there is no single nice global coordinate chart.

## The Error That Makes EqF Work

Once the observer lives on the group, the paper defines the global estimation error as

$$
e = \phi(\hat{X}^{-1}, \xi).
$$

This deserves a pause.

The error is not "true state minus estimate." It is the true state, pulled back by the inverse of the estimated symmetry action. If the estimate is perfect, then the pulled-back true state lands exactly at the chosen origin:

$$
e = \xi^\circ.
$$

That is the point around which the filter will linearize.

This is the heart of the EqF story:

- choose an origin once
- define error relative to that origin using symmetry
- linearize the error dynamics there

Instead of linearizing around the moving estimate in whatever coordinates happen to be convenient, the paper linearizes a geometrically meaningful error around a fixed point.

## Why the Fixed Origin Matters

The authors then write the error in local coordinates

$$
\varepsilon = \vartheta(e),
$$

where $\vartheta$ is a coordinate chart around the origin $\xi^\circ$.

The pre-observer error dynamics linearize to

$$
\dot{\varepsilon} = A_t^\circ \varepsilon + O(\|\varepsilon\|^2).
$$

This looks ordinary, but the construction is doing something subtle. The matrix $A_t^\circ$ comes from a linearization at the **fixed origin**, not from repeatedly re-linearizing arbitrary Euclidean coordinates of the state.

That matters because it lets the geometry and symmetry carry much more of the burden. In good cases, the linearization error becomes much smaller than what a naive EKF would see.

And in the special Lie-group, group-affine setting, the paper shows EqF reduces to the IEKF viewpoint, where the deterministic error dynamics can even become exactly linear.

## The Filter Equations

Once the linearized error model is available, the rest looks reassuringly familiar.

The EqF uses:

- an observer evolving on the group
- a correction term built from the measurement residual
- a Riccati equation for a covariance-like matrix $\Sigma$

At a high level, it is still a Kalman-style design:

- propagate the estimate
- propagate the uncertainty
- use linearized error/output models to compute a gain
- apply the correction geometrically

So the EqF is not "Kalman filtering thrown away and replaced by pure geometry." It is better thought of as:

> Kalman filtering, but with the error and linearization built from symmetry instead of from arbitrary coordinates.

## The Most Interesting Part: EqF$^\star$

The paper's nicest surprise, in my opinion, is not just the group-valued observer. It is the improved output linearization.

Normally, if we linearize the measurement residual in the error coordinates, we get something of the form

$$
\tilde{y} = C_t \varepsilon + O(\|\varepsilon\|^2).
$$

That is standard first-order linearization behavior.

But when the output is equivariant and the error coordinates are chosen as normal coordinates, the authors derive a better approximation:

$$
\tilde{y} = C_t^\star \varepsilon + O(\|\varepsilon\|^3).
$$

That is the key step behind **EqF$^\star$**.

This is a big deal because the update step is where a filter absorbs information from measurements. If the measurement model is locally more accurate, the correction can be more trustworthy, especially during transients or when the estimate is not yet very close.

The paper's experiments show exactly that: EqF$^\star$ consistently beats the plain EqF, which in turn beats the EKF.

## The Sphere Example in Plain English

The motivating example is worth understanding because it makes the abstract machinery concrete.

The state is a unit direction vector

$$
\eta \in \mathbb{S}^2.
$$

The measured angular velocity $\Omega \in \mathbb{R}^3$ drives the dynamics

$$
\dot{\eta} = -\Omega^\times \eta,
$$

and the magnetometer output is simply the direction itself, up to scale:

$$
y = c_m \eta.
$$

Now the geometry:

- the state space is the sphere $\mathbb{S}^2$
- the symmetry group is $\mathrm{SO}(3)$
- the group acts on the sphere by rotation

The sphere is not a Lie group, so an IEKF on the state space is not the natural tool here. But $\mathbb{S}^2$ is a homogeneous space under $\mathrm{SO}(3)$, which makes it a perfect EqF example.

The observer stores a rotation $\hat{R} \in \mathrm{SO}(3)$, not directly a point on the sphere. Pick the origin to be the first basis vector $e_1$. Then the estimate is

$$
\hat{\eta} = \hat{R}^\top e_1.
$$

That is a very natural geometric picture: $\hat{R}$ is the rotation that carries the reference direction to the estimated direction.

The error becomes

$$
e = \hat{R}\eta,
$$

which equals the origin $e_1$ when the estimate is correct.

From there, the paper chooses normal coordinates around $e_1$, computes the EqF matrices, and runs the filter.

The outcome is exactly what the theory predicts:

- the EqF outperforms a carefully designed EKF
- the EqF$^\star$ outperforms the EqF again

This is important because the benchmark EKF in the paper is not a straw man. The authors give it a thoughtful embedded-state implementation with a sphere constraint. EqF still comes out ahead.

## How EqF Relates to IEKF

One of the easiest ways to place the EqF is this:

- **IEKF**: best thought of as the symmetry-aware Kalman filter for systems on Lie groups with the right invariance or group-affine structure
- **EqF**: the broader framework that keeps the same philosophy but works on homogeneous spaces

So EqF is not competing with IEKF so much as containing it.

The paper's Appendix B makes this precise: if the state space is itself a Lie group, the origin is chosen to be the identity, the local coordinates are logarithmic coordinates, and the system is group affine, then the EqF specializes to the IEKF.

That is a reassuring result. It says the EqF is not inventing a different universe. It is extending the same symmetry-based logic to settings where IEKF no longer applies directly.

## EqF in GTSAM

At this point, it is worth asking what EqF looks like in an actual software library.

One of the nicest things about the current GTSAM work is that it does not present EqF as an isolated one-off class. It treats it as part of a larger **manifold filtering hierarchy**. In the same way that the GTSAM blog post on manifold Kalman filters walks from `ManifoldEKF` to `LieGroupEKF` to `InvariantEKF`, the EqF fits naturally as the next symmetry-aware step rather than as a completely separate design.

In GTSAM, the generic implementation lives in [`gtsam/navigation/EquivariantFilter.h`](https://github.com/borglab/gtsam/blob/develop/gtsam/navigation/EquivariantFilter.h). The class is templated on:

- a manifold state type `M`
- a symmetry functor `Symmetry`

and it inherits from `ManifoldEKF<M>`.

That design is already a big clue about how the implementation thinks about the filter:

- the estimate lives on the manifold
- the covariance lives in a tangent space
- the EqF-specific contribution is the symmetry machinery that defines the group estimate, the action, the lifted dynamics, and the innovation lift

In other words, GTSAM is not reimplementing “yet another EKF” from scratch. It is reusing the manifold EKF backbone and adding the specific pieces that make the filter equivariant.

### What the Generic Class Is Doing

The generic `EquivariantFilter` tracks both:

- a reference state `xi_ref` on the manifold, which plays the role of the fixed EqF origin, and
- a group element `g`, which is the lifted estimate

The estimated manifold state is recovered by applying the group action to the reference state.

This mirrors the paper very closely. The implementation also precomputes the differential of the action at the identity and its pseudoinverse, which is then used as the **innovation lift** during updates. That is exactly the computational version of the paper's idea that a correction is first computed in manifold error coordinates and then lifted back to the group.

Another nice detail is that the class supports two prediction styles:

- an **automatic** mode, where it computes the error Jacobian from the lift and the input orbit
- an **explicit** mode, where the user provides the linearized error Jacobian and process covariance directly

That is a practical choice. It keeps the generic formulation close to the theory, while still giving room for hand-derived Jacobians when those are clearer or more efficient.

### Why the GTSAM Implementation Is Useful

There are at least four reasons the GTSAM implementation is more than just a translation of the paper.

#### 1. It puts EqF into the same conceptual ladder as the other EKF variants

One of the hardest things about learning geometric filtering is not any single formula. It is understanding how all the variants relate.

GTSAM helps by making the progression explicit:

- `ManifoldEKF`: state on a manifold, covariance in tangent coordinates
- `LieGroupEKF`: prediction specialized to Lie groups
- `InvariantEKF`: exploit invariant prediction structure
- `EquivariantFilter`: go beyond invariance and use a symmetry action more generally

That hierarchy makes EqF feel much less mysterious. It becomes the natural continuation of the same story rather than a separate branch of estimation theory.

#### 2. It uses GTSAM's manifold and Lie-group abstractions directly

This is probably the biggest engineering benefit.

In a research script, it is very easy to bury the geometry inside ad hoc matrices, hand-rolled retractions, and frame-conversion code. GTSAM gives you typed geometric objects such as `Rot3`, `Pose3`, and `Unit3`, plus the manifold operations and Jacobian conventions that go with them.

That matters because EqF is all about getting the geometry right. A library that already understands manifolds, tangent spaces, retractions, and Lie groups is a much better home for EqF than a pile of raw NumPy arrays.

#### 3. It separates the generic EqF plumbing from model-specific code

The generic class only needs a few principled ingredients:

- the state manifold
- the symmetry action
- the lift
- the input orbit
- the measurement model

Once those are defined, the rest of the update logic looks like an ordinary GTSAM EKF.

This separation is a real win. It means you can focus on the symmetry of your particular problem rather than rewriting Kalman gain logic, covariance updates, and Joseph-form bookkeeping every time.

#### 4. It comes with tests and worked examples

This is easy to overlook, but it matters a lot for a concept as abstract as EqF.

There is a simple attitude-only test in [`gtsam/navigation/tests/testEquivariantFilter.cpp`](https://github.com/borglab/gtsam/blob/develop/gtsam/navigation/tests/testEquivariantFilter.cpp) that uses:

- `Unit3` as the physical state on $\mathbb{S}^2$
- `Rot3` as the symmetry group
- a right action of `SO(3)` on the sphere

That example is valuable because it is very close to the single-bearing story from the EqF paper. It shows that the implementation really is generic: the class is not secretly tied to one special application.

Then there is a higher-level example in [`examples/AbcEquivariantFilterExample.cpp`](https://github.com/borglab/gtsam/blob/develop/examples/AbcEquivariantFilterExample.cpp), together with the specialized wrapper [`gtsam_unstable/geometry/ABCEquivariantFilter.h`](https://github.com/borglab/gtsam/blob/develop/gtsam_unstable/geometry/ABCEquivariantFilter.h).

That ABC wrapper is especially nice for teaching, because it turns the fully generic EqF interface into something much friendlier:

- `predict(omega, inputCovariance, dt)`
- `update(y, d, R, cal_idx)`
- accessors such as `attitude()`, `bias()`, and `calibration(i)`

So readers can learn the geometric idea once in the generic class, then see what a user-facing problem-specific EqF API looks like in practice.

## Why This Matters for Learning

I think this is the real payoff of having EqF in GTSAM.

The original paper is mathematically elegant, but for many readers it still feels one level removed from implementation. GTSAM closes that gap. It shows:

- how the paper's reference state becomes an actual stored object
- how the innovation lift becomes a concrete pseudoinverse in code
- how the group action becomes a functor
- how prediction and update fit into the same EKF workflow as the rest of the library

That makes EqF much easier to trust and much easier to experiment with.

If you are learning this material, the combination I would recommend is:

1. read the EqF paper for the geometry and derivation
2. read the GTSAM manifold-KF hierarchy post for the broader filtering picture
3. inspect `EquivariantFilter.h` and the attitude-only test to see how the abstractions land in code
4. then move to the higher-level ABC example or notebook-style demos

That path makes the theory feel a lot less abstract.

## A Practical Recipe for Reading or Implementing EqF

If you want to use the paper as an implementation guide, here is the checklist hidden inside the notation:

1. Identify the symmetry group $G$ and its action $\phi$ on the state space.
2. Check how the inputs transform, giving the input action $\psi$.
3. Construct an equivariant lift $\Lambda$.
4. Check whether the output is also equivariant through some action $\rho$.
5. Choose an origin $\xi^\circ$ and local coordinates around it.
6. Define the observer on the group, not directly on the state space.
7. Derive the error coordinates $\varepsilon$ and linearized matrices.
8. Use the standard EqF output linearization, or the EqF$^\star$ one if output equivariance is available.

That is the workflow the authors summarize in the appendix, and it is the right way to mentally organize the paper.

## What the Paper Is Really Contributing

After reading the paper closely, I think its main contributions are these:

### 1. A filter design on homogeneous spaces

This is the headline contribution. EqF works when the state has symmetry but is not itself a Lie group.

### 2. A symmetry-derived global error

The error is constructed from the group action instead of from arbitrary subtraction in coordinates.

### 3. Linearization at a fixed origin

This gives a cleaner and often better-behaved error model than repeated coordinate-based linearization around the moving estimate.

### 4. A higher-order output approximation

EqF$^\star$ uses output equivariance to improve the measurement linearization from second-order error to third-order error.

That last point is the most technically distinctive part of the paper, and the simulation section shows it is not just a theoretical curiosity.

## A Good Mental Model to Keep

Here is the mental model I would recommend carrying forward:

> The EqF is an error-state Kalman filter where the error is chosen by symmetry.

That framing makes the paper much easier to digest.

You still have:

- a prediction model
- a measurement residual
- a linearized system
- a Riccati equation
- a gain-driven correction

What changes is that the "right" coordinates are not guessed from convenience. They are induced by the geometry of the system.

## Final Takeaway

The EqF paper is worth reading not because it replaces everything we already know about filtering, but because it sharpens a very useful principle:

> if a system has symmetry, the filter should use it everywhere it can: in the state representation, in the error definition, and in the linearization

That is the real thread connecting EKF, IEKF, and EqF.

The standard EKF linearizes whatever coordinates we hand it. The IEKF chooses an invariant error on a Lie group. The EqF pushes that same idea further, to homogeneous spaces and equivariant outputs.

If you are comfortable with the IEKF already, the EqF is the natural next step. If you are new to both, EqF is a great paper to read slowly with one question in mind:

> What is the symmetry of my estimation problem, and what would happen if I built the error from that first?
